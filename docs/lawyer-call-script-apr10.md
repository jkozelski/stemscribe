## Part 2: The Core Product (20 min)

### 2A. Upload + Separate Model

"Users upload an audio file — an MP3 or WAV they own — and my AI separates it into individual instruments: vocals, guitar, bass, drums, piano. Then they can practice along, mute parts, slow it down. *She should mention DMCA safe harbor, user responsibility, ToS language.*

## Part 4: AI Transcription (15 min)

### 4A. What It Actually Does

"The name StemScriber comes from stems + transcriber. The transcription side is limited right now — for popular songs, the tabs already exist online so we pull from a tab database. The AI transcription is mainly useful for live recordings and obscure material where no existing charts exist. A student brings in a live recording from a show, and the AI can listen to the separated piano stem and generate sheet music, or chart out drum notation."

*Frame it as a teaching tool for edge cases, not mass transcription of copyrighted catalogs.*

### 4B. The Legal Question

"Is AI-generated transcription of copyrighted music an issue? If a student uploads a live Grateful Dead recording and my AI transcribes the piano part into sheet music — is that a derivative work or transformative use?"

*This is the one genuinely open legal question. No competitor has been tested on this.*

### 4C. Print License Question

"Some of my research suggests that printed arrangements of copyrighted music require a 'print license' from publishers. Does auto-generated notation fall into that category? Or is it different because it's AI-generated from audio, not a human arrangement?"
