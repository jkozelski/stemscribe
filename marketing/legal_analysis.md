## 1. How Competitors Legally Operate

### Moises.ai

Moises is the closest direct competitor and the best model for StemScriber's legal posture. Key elements of their approach:

- **User responsibility model:** Users affirm they own or have legal rights to any audio they upload. Moises' ToS states users "are solely responsible for their User Content and the consequences of providing User Content via the Service."
- **No hosting of copyrighted music:** Moises does not host a library of songs. Users must provide their own audio files. They explicitly block streaming service URLs (Spotify, Apple Music, etc.) to avoid facilitating piracy.
- **DMCA compliance:** Full DMCA safe harbor under 17 U.S.C. § 512. Designated Copyright Agent registered with the U.S. Copyright Office (Moises Systems, Inc., Salt Lake City, UT).
- **Ownership of outputs:** Moises does not claim ownership of separated stems or other outputs — ownership follows the input audio's rights.
- **Tool framing:** Positions itself as a "musician's practice tool" — emphasis on education, practice, and creative production rather than piracy or redistribution.

### LALAL.AI

- Similar user-responsibility model. Users upload their own files.
- No song library or streaming integration.
- Based in Estonia (EU jurisdiction), but serves US users.
- Less publicly detailed legal posture than Moises, but functionally identical approach.

### Common Legal Framework

Both services operate under the same core legal theory: **they are neutral audio processing tools**. They don't store or distribute copyrighted content — they process audio that users provide and return modified versions. This is analogous to a photocopier: the manufacturer isn't liable for what users copy.

## 2. Legal Precedents Protecting Audio Processing Tools

### Sony Corp. v. Universal City Studios (1984) — The "Betamax" Case

**464 U.S. 417 (1984)** — The foundational precedent for technology companies.

The Supreme Court ruled that Sony was not liable for contributory copyright infringement for manufacturing VCRs, even though VCRs could be used to copy copyrighted television programs. The key holding:

> "The sale of copying equipment, like the sale of other articles of commerce, does not constitute contributory infringement if the product is **widely used for legitimate, unobjectionable purposes**, or need merely be **capable of substantial noninfringing uses**."

**Application to StemScriber:** Stem separation has abundant legitimate uses:
- Musicians practicing along with isolated instruments
- Music students studying arrangements and instrumentation
- Producers creating authorized remixes
- Podcasters/YouTubers isolating vocals for commentary
- Karaoke creation from songs the user owns
- Accessibility (isolating vocals for hearing-impaired listeners)
- Sound engineers analyzing mixes

The "substantial noninfringing use" standard is easily met.

### MGM Studios v. Grokster (2005)

**545 U.S. 913 (2005)** — Limits the Betamax defense.

The Supreme Court held that **inducement** of infringement can create liability, even for a tool with substantial noninfringing uses. Grokster was liable because it **actively promoted** its service as a way to download copyrighted music for free, replacing Napster.

**Application to StemScriber:** This case highlights what NOT to do:
- Never market StemScriber as a way to "get free music" or "rip songs"
- Never encourage users to process music they don't own
- Never build features specifically designed to facilitate piracy (e.g., YouTube ripping, streaming service integration)

### Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music (1994) — "Transformative Use"

**510 U.S. 569 (1994)** — Established the modern "transformative use" framework for fair use.

A use is more likely fair when it "adds something new, with a different purpose or different character" rather than merely superseding the original. The Court emphasized that commercial use doesn't automatically defeat fair use if the use is sufficiently transformative.

**Application to StemScriber:** Stem separation is arguably transformative — it takes a complete mix and deconstructs it into component parts for analysis, practice, or creative reuse. The output (individual stems) serves a fundamentally different purpose than the original (a complete song for listening).

### Andy Warhol Foundation v. Goldsmith (2023)

**598 U.S. 508 (2023)** — Narrowed the transformative use doctrine.

The Supreme Court held that when the original work and the new use share the same purpose (in that case, magazine illustrations), the first fair use factor weighs against the copier. The "transformative" label requires more than just adding new expression — the new use must have a genuinely different purpose.

**Application to StemScriber:** This actually helps StemScriber's case. Separated stems serve a different purpose (practice, education, production) than the original song (entertainment/listening). A vocal-removed track for karaoke practice is functionally different from the original commercial recording.

## 3. Is Stem Separation Fair Use?

### The Four-Factor Test (17 U.S.C. § 107)

#### Factor 1: Purpose and Character of the Use

**Arguments in favor:**
- Stem separation is transformative — it deconstructs a work for analysis, education, and creative reuse
- Primary uses are practice, education, and production — not replacement of the original
- The output (isolated stems) is not a substitute for the original product (complete song)
- Analogous to a student transcribing a song by ear — digitally assisted learning

**Arguments against:**
- Commercial service charging for the processing
- Some users may use stems to create unauthorized derivative works
- An isolated vocal track could potentially substitute for purchasing an a cappella version

#### Factor 2: Nature of the Copyrighted Work

- Music is creative/expressive, which typically weighs against fair use
- However, users are processing works they already possess, not accessing new copies

#### Factor 3: Amount Used

- The entire work is processed, which typically weighs against fair use
- But the output is not the entire work — it's component parts, each incomplete on its own
- Analogous to quoting an entire short poem for criticism (sometimes permitted)

#### Factor 4: Market Impact

**Arguments in favor:**
- Stem separation doesn't replace the market for the original song
- No one buys a StemScriber subscription instead of buying songs
- The tool requires the user to already have the audio file

**Arguments against:**
- Could undercut the market for officially licensed stems/multitracks
- Some artists sell stem packs; unauthorized separation could compete with that market

### Overall Assessment

Stem separation as a process likely qualifies as fair use when:
- The user owns or has rights to the source audio
- The purpose is practice, education, or authorized creative production
- The separated stems are not redistributed commercially

The tool provider (StemScriber) has even stronger protection than individual users because StemScriber itself doesn't choose what to process — users do. This puts StemScriber in the same position as a photocopier or VCR manufacturer.

**The key academic reference:** The Northwestern Journal of Technology and Intellectual Property published "AI Stem Extraction: A Creative Tool or Facilitator of Mass Infringement?" (2022), which analyzes stem extraction through all four fair use factors and concludes that the legality depends heavily on the end use rather than the separation process itself.

## 4. Tabs and Sheet Music: How UG, Songsterr, and Chordify Operate {#4-tabs-and-sheet-music}

### Ultimate Guitar

- **Licensing model:** UG secured a licensing arrangement with the Harry Fox Agency (HFA) in 2010, covering lyrics display, title search, and tablature display with download/print capabilities. HFA represents over 44,000 publishers who can opt in.
- **Additional deals:** By 2019, UG had direct agreements with Sony/ATV Music, EMI, Alfred, Hal Leonard, Peermusic, Faber, and Music Sales.
- **Revenue sharing:** UG pays royalties to publishers through these licensing arrangements.
- **User-generated content:** Most tabs on UG are user-submitted transcriptions (interpretations, not copies of official sheet music). UG moderates and hosts these under their licensing umbrella.

### Songsterr

- **AI-generated tabs:** Songsterr recently launched an AI tab generation feature that creates tabs from YouTube audio links.
- **Licensing:** Operates under similar licensing frameworks as UG, though less publicly documented.
- **Premium model:** Free access to basic tabs; premium features behind paywall.

### Chordify

- **Legal position:** Chordify argues that chord progressions themselves are not copyrightable — they are not "innovative or sufficiently unique enough to be copyrighted on their own." This is legally sound: chord progressions are generally considered musical building blocks, not protectable expression.
- **No publisher licensing:** Unlike UG, Chordify does not appear to maintain licensing deals with publishers.
- **Audio source:** Chordify analyzes audio from YouTube/Spotify (linked, not downloaded) and extracts only chord symbols — not melodies, lyrics, or detailed notation.

### Key Takeaway for StemScriber

Generating tablature/sheet music is legally murkier than stem separation because it creates a written representation of a copyrighted composition. Different approaches carry different risk levels:

| Approach | Risk Level | Notes |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| Chord detection only | Low | Chord progressions generally not copyrightable |
| User-generated tabs (hosted) | Medium | Requires publisher licensing (UG model) |
| Automated full transcription (melody + rhythm) | Higher | Creates derivative work of the composition |
| AI-generated tabs from user audio | Medium | User provides source; tool is neutral processor |

StemScriber's transcription feature (audio → MIDI → Guitar Pro) falls into the "automated full transcription" category. Risk mitigation: ensure the user provides the audio, the transcription is for personal use, and no transcriptions are publicly hosted or shared.

## 5. User-Generated vs. Automated Transcriptions {#5-user-generated-vs-automated-transcriptions}

### User-Generated Transcriptions

- A person listening to a song and writing down the notes/chords is creating an **interpretation** — their own creative expression of what they hear
- Courts have not clearly ruled on whether user-generated tabs are derivative works or independent expressions
- The music industry sent cease-and-desist letters to tab sites in the mid-2000s (MusicPublisherAssociation targeted OLGA, MXTabs, and others), leading most to either shut down or seek licensing (as UG did)
- The prevailing industry consensus is that tabs require licensing, even if this hasn't been fully tested in court

### Automated/AI Transcriptions

- An AI-generated transcription is more clearly a derivative work because it's a more literal representation of the composition
- The AI doesn't add creative interpretation — it attempts to accurately reproduce the underlying musical content
- However, the transcription is created from audio the user already possesses, for the user's private use
- Analogy: using speech-to-text on an audiobook you purchased. The resulting text is a derivative work, but creating it for personal use from media you own is likely fair use

### Legal Gray Area

Neither category has been definitively adjudicated in US courts. The practical reality:
- Publishers have not pursued individual users who transcribe songs for personal use
- Publishers have pursued platforms that host and distribute transcriptions at scale without licensing
- The risk sits with the platform, not the individual user

**StemScriber's position:** StemScriber generates transcriptions from user-provided audio, for the user's personal use. Transcriptions are not hosted publicly or shared between users. This is the lowest-risk posture for automated transcription.

## 6. Lawsuits and Legal Challenges

### Directly Relevant Cases

**No lawsuits have been filed against stem separation services as of March 2026.** Neither Moises, LALAL.AI, nor any similar service has been sued for copyright infringement related to their stem separation functionality.

This is significant — these services have operated for years, process millions of songs, and the music industry has not pursued legal action against them. This suggests the industry either:
1. Accepts the tool-provider defense (Betamax doctrine)
2. Doesn't view stem separation tools as a meaningful threat
3. Is waiting for a strategic test case

### Adjacent Cases

**RIAA v. Suno / RIAA v. Udio (2024):**
- Record labels (Sony, UMG, Warner) sued AI music generation services Suno and Udio for training their models on copyrighted recordings without authorization.
- Suno admitted using copyrighted music for training and argued fair use.
- UMG settled with Udio in 2025.
- **Relevance to StemScriber:** These cases target AI music *generation* (creating new music that sounds like copyrighted works), NOT audio processing/separation. StemScriber doesn't generate new music — it processes existing audio that users provide. Different legal theory entirely.

**Stem-related sampling disputes (2024-2025):**
- Multiple lawsuits between labels and producers over unauthorized use of stems, loops, and beats in hit songs.
- These involve the *use* of separated stems in new commercial releases, not the separation tools themselves.
- **Relevance:** Reinforces that the legal risk is in how stems are *used*, not in the act of separation.

**Music Publisher Association v. Tab Sites (2006-2010):**
- MPA sent cease-and-desist letters to guitar tab sites including OLGA, MXTabs, Guitar Tab Universe, and others.
- Most sites shut down or removed content. UG survived by negotiating licensing deals.
- **Relevance:** Establishes that hosting/distributing transcriptions of copyrighted music at scale requires licensing. StemScriber should NOT host a public library of transcriptions.

## 7. Recommendations for StemScriber

### Tier 1: Must-Do (Legal Essentials)

1. **DMCA Policy:** Publish a DMCA policy page with designated agent info. [DONE — see dmca.html]

2. **Register DMCA Agent:** File with the U.S. Copyright Office at copyright.gov/dmca-directory. Fee is $6. Must be renewed every 3 years.

3. **Terms of Service — User Responsibility:** Include clear provisions that:
   - Users must own or have rights to any audio they upload
   - Users are solely responsible for their content and how they use outputs
   - Users must not redistribute separated stems or transcriptions of copyrighted works
   - Violation results in account termination

4. **Repeat Infringer Policy:** Adopt, publicize, and implement a repeat infringer policy (three-strikes or similar). Required for DMCA safe harbor.

5. **No Streaming Integration:** Never allow users to input Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube Music, or other streaming URLs. Require file uploads only. This is what Moises does and it's a critical legal firewall.

6. **No Public Content Library:** Never host a searchable library of stems or transcriptions. All processed content should be private to the user who uploaded it.

### Tier 2: Strongly Recommended

7. **No Permanent Storage of Source Audio:** Process audio, return results, and delete the source file. Don't build a library of users' uploaded songs. Minimizes exposure.

8. **Rate Limiting:** Implement reasonable processing limits to prevent bulk/industrial use that could look like a piracy operation.

9. **Positioning:** Always market StemScriber as a practice/education/production tool. Never use language that implies it's for getting free music, ripping songs, or bypassing copyright.

10. **Block Known Piracy Sources:** If feasible, detect and reject audio files that appear to be direct rips from streaming services (e.g., files with streaming service metadata).

11. **Copyright Notice in UI:** Add a brief notice near the upload area: "By uploading, you confirm you have the right to process this audio."

### Tier 3: Future Considerations

12. **Publisher Licensing (if hosting transcriptions publicly):** If StemScriber ever adds a public tab/transcription library (like UG), licensing deals with HFA or individual publishers would be necessary. For now, keeping transcriptions private to each user avoids this requirement.

13. **Content Identification:** Consider implementing audio fingerprinting to detect and flag obvious copyrighted content if the platform grows large enough to attract industry attention.

14. **Legal Entity:** Operate StemScriber through an LLC or corporation to limit personal liability.

15. **Insurance:** Consider errors & omissions (E&O) or intellectual property insurance as the business scales.

### Risk Assessment Summary

| Feature | Risk Level | Mitigation |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| Stem separation | Low | Betamax doctrine + user-provided audio |
| MIDI transcription (private) | Low-Medium | User-provided audio, private output, personal use |
| Karaoke mode | Low | Transformative use for practice |
| Tab/notation display (private) | Low-Medium | Generated from user's own audio |
| Public tab library | HIGH | Would require publisher licensing |
| Streaming URL input | HIGH | Never implement |
| Storing/sharing stems | Medium-High | Delete after processing |

### Bottom Line

StemScriber's current architecture — user uploads audio, gets stems + transcription for personal use, nothing is hosted publicly — is the safest legal posture. This mirrors what Moises and LALAL.AI do, and neither has faced legal challenge. The Betamax doctrine protects the tool, and the DMCA safe harbor protects the platform, as long as StemScriber:

1. Registers a DMCA agent
2. Responds to takedown notices
3. Implements a repeat infringer policy
4. Doesn't actively encourage infringement
5. Keeps user content private
